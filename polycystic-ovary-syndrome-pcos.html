Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)

A plain-language look at one of the most common hormonal conditions in women — how it can show up, why it is often missed, and what a root-cause approach means.

By Ilona Y. Bleaman, PA‑C, MHPE · Reviewed by the Internally Balanced team: pending

Book a ConsultationPatient Portal

Polycystic ovary syndrome, or PCOS, is one of the more common hormonal conditions that can affect women during the reproductive years. Despite how frequently it is discussed, it is often under-recognized or misunderstood — partly because it looks different from person to person. Learning the general patterns can help you have a more informed conversation with a qualified provider about whether an evaluation makes sense for you.

What is PCOS?

PCOS is a hormonal and metabolic condition, not simply a problem with the ovaries. The name can be misleading: despite the word "polycystic," it is not defined by cysts in the way many people assume. Instead, clinicians generally look at a combination of features — such as irregular or absent ovulation, signs of higher levels of certain hormones sometimes called androgens, and specific findings on ultrasound — and consider them together while ruling out other explanations. Because it is a syndrome, the way it is recognized relies on a pattern rather than a single test.

Signs and patterns to be aware of

PCOS can present in many ways, and no single sign confirms or rules it out. Some patterns that lead people to seek evaluation include:

  • Irregular, infrequent or unpredictable menstrual cycles
  • Difficulty with ovulation, which can affect fertility for some individuals
  • Skin changes such as acne, or changes in hair growth or hair thinning
  • Weight changes or difficulty with weight despite genuine effort
  • Fatigue, mood changes, or a general sense that something is off
Every one of these signs can have other causes, and many people with one or two of them do not have PCOS. That overlap is exactly why self-diagnosis is unreliable and a professional evaluation matters. Only a qualified clinician can determine whether these patterns fit a diagnosis in your case.

Why PCOS is often missed

Because PCOS varies so much between individuals, it can go unrecognized for years. Someone with regular-seeming cycles may still have it; someone with irregular cycles may have a different cause. Symptoms are frequently attributed to lifestyle alone, and the condition does not announce itself with a single obvious sign. This is one reason a careful, whole-picture assessment — rather than a single test in isolation — tends to be more useful.

A root-cause approach

PCOS is closely tied to how the body handles hormones and, for many people, how it regulates blood sugar and metabolism. A root-cause approach means looking beyond the surface symptoms to understand the underlying drivers at work in an individual, rather than only managing one symptom at a time. In practice, this means taking a thorough history, considering appropriate evaluation, and thinking about how factors like metabolism, stress, sleep and nutrition may fit together for that person.

What follows an evaluation is highly individual. Depending on someone's goals — whether that is regulating cycles, addressing specific symptoms, supporting fertility or improving overall well-being — a plan is tailored to them and discussed openly, including the reasoning, options and what to expect. There is no single protocol that fits everyone, and any approach is chosen with the individual and reviewed over time.

When to seek an evaluation

If you have noticed irregular cycles, skin or hair changes, difficulty with weight, or concerns about fertility, it is reasonable to talk with a qualified provider. Early recognition can help you understand what is happening and make informed choices. Avoid diagnosing yourself from online checklists, and avoid starting supplements or medications on your own — PCOS care benefits from individual evaluation and appropriate follow-up.
