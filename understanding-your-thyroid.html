Understanding Your Thyroid

A plain-language look at the small gland that influences energy, weight, mood and more — plus why some people feel off even when a lab result is labeled "normal."

By Ilona Y. Bleaman, PA‑C, MHPE · Published March 15, 2024 · Updated July 16, 2026

Book a ConsultationPatient Portal

The thyroid is a small, butterfly-shaped gland at the front of the neck, but its influence reaches nearly every system in the body. It helps set the pace of your metabolism — the rate at which your cells use energy — which is why thyroid changes can show up as fatigue, weight changes, temperature sensitivity, mood shifts and more. Understanding how it works can help you have a more informed conversation about your own symptoms.

What the thyroid does

The thyroid produces hormones that act as messengers throughout the body. The two most discussed are often abbreviated T4 and T3. In simple terms, the gland releases mostly T4, and the body converts a portion of it into the more active T3 where it is needed. These hormones help regulate how quickly your body uses energy, and they play a role in heart rate, digestion, temperature regulation and cognitive function.

The thyroid does not act alone. It is guided by the brain through a signaling loop. The pituitary gland releases a hormone commonly called TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone), which tells the thyroid how much to produce. When thyroid hormone levels are low, TSH tends to rise; when they are high, TSH tends to fall. This feedback system is why TSH is such a commonly used screening test.

When the thyroid is over- or under-active

Broadly, thyroid function can drift in two directions, and the general patterns are worth recognizing — while remembering that symptoms overlap with many other conditions.

Underactive (hypothyroid) patterns

When the thyroid produces too little hormone, the body's pace can slow. People sometimes describe fatigue, feeling cold, dry skin, constipation, low mood, difficulty concentrating or unexplained weight changes. These experiences are nonspecific, which is exactly why they should be evaluated rather than self-diagnosed.

Overactive (hyperthyroid) patterns

When the thyroid produces too much hormone, the body's pace can speed up. This may show up as a racing heart, feeling warm or sweaty, anxiousness, trouble sleeping, tremor or weight changes. As with the underactive pattern, these symptoms have many possible causes and warrant professional assessment.

What a "normal" lab may miss

Many thyroid evaluations begin with a single TSH test. That test is useful, but a result that falls inside the standard reference range does not automatically mean everything about the thyroid is fully understood — especially when symptoms persist. There are a few reasons for this.

  • A single marker offers a limited view. Depending on the situation, a clinician may consider additional measures — for example, thyroid hormone levels themselves or thyroid antibodies — to build a fuller picture.
  • Reference ranges describe a population, not necessarily your individual optimum, and interpretation can depend on your history and symptoms.
  • Levels can fluctuate, and factors such as timing, medications, illness, pregnancy and other conditions can influence results.
  • Symptoms and labs do not always move together, so how you feel is part of the assessment — not an afterthought.
This does not mean a normal result is "wrong," and it does not mean everyone needs extensive testing. It means results are best interpreted in context, by a qualified clinician, alongside your history and how you actually feel. What the right evaluation looks like is individual.

A whole-person view

The thyroid is influenced by, and connected to, other parts of your physiology — including stress, nutrition, sleep and other hormones. Because of this, thyroid symptoms are best understood as one part of a larger picture rather than an isolated number. A careful evaluation looks at how the pieces fit together before drawing conclusions or considering any plan, which is always tailored to the individual.

What to do if you have concerns

If you are noticing persistent changes in energy, weight, mood, temperature tolerance or other areas, the most useful next step is a conversation with a qualified provider who can review your history, examine you, and determine what evaluation — if any — makes sense for you. Avoid starting, stopping or adjusting thyroid medication or supplements on your own, as thyroid care requires appropriate monitoring over time.
